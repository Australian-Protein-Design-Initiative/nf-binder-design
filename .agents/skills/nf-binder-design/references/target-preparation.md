# Target Preparation and Viability Assessment

## Assessing Target Viability

The purpose of these questions is to determine the viability of a proposed protein design project and guide target selection and preparation, based on:
- The data you have available (3D structures, understanding of function, interactions, and localisation)
- The availability of an assay (biophysical, *in vitro*, or *in vivo*)
- The goals and expected outcomes of the design campaign
- The current state of the art in methodology
- The capabilities and experience of the staff and collaborators

### Protein Design Goal
Are you considering:
- protein binder design
- antibody design
- nanobody design
- (cyclic)-peptide design
- peptides or proteins with non-natural modifications
- protein-ligand binder design
- DNA/RNA binder design
- enzyme design

`nf-binder-design` is currently tailored for protein (and [cyclic]-peptide) binder design against protein targets. Design of non-native peptides, design against DNA/RNA, design of small molecule binding proteins and enzyme design is currently out of scope.

**Key questions:**
- Consider - is this a good experimental/clinical target?
- What characteristics should the designed binders have to be better/cheaper/safer/unique relative to existing tools or therapies?
- Are you intending to design an entirely new *de novo* protein/peptide, or are you optimizing an existing binder or enzyme?
- If you are optimizing an existing protein, do you intend to optimize for:
  - Binding affinity
  - Thermostability
  - Immunogenicity
  - Enzyme activity
  - *In vivo* lifetime, bioavailability, pharmacokinetics, localisation / tissue distribution?
  - Other?

### Existing Data
- Have you searched the literature to find all experimental and predicted target structures and gain an understanding of the structural biology of your target protein?
- Do you have a 3D structure of the target?
- Is this a:
  - Cryo-EM structure
  - X-ray crystal structure
  - NMR structure
  - Predicted structure (AlphaFold, Boltz, Chai, etc.)
- Can you provide links to the structures in the PDB database, AlphaFold Structure Database, or similar?
- Is your native protein multimeric (homodimer, heterotetramer, etc.) or usually part of a stable protein complex?
- Is the multimeric state or complex represented in the structure coordinates provided?
- If you only have a predicted structure:
  - Inspect the associated pTM, PAE matrix, and pLDDT confidence scores - is the structure likely to be correct and well defined in the region you intend to target?
  - Do you believe this predicted structure is accurate based on other data and the current understanding of its biological function?
- Is the provided target structure complete / high quality / well-defined in the regions required for binder design? Check metrics like resolution, B-factors, missing loops, and correct multimeric state.
- Do you need to add / rebuild missing loops?
- Does your target have glycosylation sites or other known post-translational modifications, cofactors, etc.? Are their locations known? Are these represented in the 3D structure?
- In the assay or biological system, will the intended target surfaces be accessible, or how will binders get there?
- What is the length, in amino acids, of the domain(s) you intend to target?

### Wet-lab Assays
*Expression vectors for bacterial or eukaryotic expression are typically generated by gene synthesis.*

- How will the *de novo* binders be produced?
  - Recombinant bacterial expression (*E. coli*)?
  - Mammalian cell surface display?
  - Some other system?
- How will the target be produced?
- Do you have a reliable medium to high throughput assay? How will you experimentally test binding? You should expect to assay ~96 binders in a typical design campaign.

### Intellectual Property
- Do you have freedom to operate using the target structures and intended assays if the designs are ultimately commercialised?
- Will you obtain the necessary software licenses to use these pipelines commercially (e.g., RFdiffusion and BindCraft require a Rosetta license)?

## Finding and Preparing Target Structures

### Quality, Completeness, and Alternative Structures
- Ensure your target structure is of high quality and completeness in the epitope/target region. Missing loops in the binding interface will cause problems for binder generation.
- **Alternative structures**: Consider whether to use an experimental structure (X-ray, Cryo-EM) or a predicted structure (AlphaFold, Boltz-2). Using an experimental structure as a template for a new AlphaFold2 or Boltz-2 prediction can help fill in missing loops.
- Be aware that even small variations of the "same" target (experimental vs. predicted vs. alternative trimming) can change *in silico* success rates significantly.

### Selection of Hotspots
- Select hotspots based on your design goals, existing biology, and known interaction sites.
- Targeting a radial patch of surface residues on secondary structures is highly recommended.
- Surface aromatics (F, Y, W) and hydrophobic residues (I, L, M) generally make the best hotspots because they provide strong binding energy anchors, where available.
- Single residues can also work in well-defined binding sites.

- AVOID choosing hotspots near any post-translational modifications (known glycosylation sites, NAG/MAN/BMA-linked etc residues). These heteroatoms are often not present in the target coordinates, or not visible to the binder design method. Check the UniProt post-translational modification records to see if there are any high likelihood sites to avoid that may not be represented in the target coordinates.

- AVOID hotspots that are close to and discontinuous/non-native crops with exposed hydrophobic core between segments.

- If you need to target two distant patches, consider separate design campaigns for each.

### Verify Hotspot Numbering
- Different `--method`'s may use either the original PDB residue numbering (eg `bindcraft`), or 1-based residue index numbering (`boltzgen`). Check the reference material for each method to ensure you are using the correct scheme. Ensure your hotspot numbering and chain designations are correct for the input target after cropping AND method used. You may use `bin/renumber_chains.py`, update your input structure residue numbers accordingly. A mismatch here will often appear to run but design a binder against the wrong interface.

### Cropping, Trimming, and Truncating
To reduce VRAM requirements and speed up computation (especially for methods like BindCraft and BoltzGen), you can choose to crop the target protein to the essential domains, although this should be done cautiously as it is not always possible without introducing unwanted exposed hydrophobic core residues or distortions during binder design. Not all structures need to be, or can be, cropped.

**Best Practices for Trimming:**
1. **Manual Inspection**: Use PyMOL or ChimeraX to manually inspect the structure and remove domains that are not required for binding.
2. **Realistic Trims**: Trim at realistic points like domain boundaries or hinge residues (e.g., Gly, Pro), which can often be identified by high B-factors or low pLDDT scores. Remove flexible ends and large flexible loops if they are not involved in the binding interface.
3. **Avoid Unrealistic Cropping**: Avoid "unrealistic" or extreme cropping (e.g., splitting a GPCR helix in half) that distorts the target or exposes the hydrophobic core. Exposing the hydrophobic core will cause models to preferentially design binders against the exposed core rather than your desired hotspot region.

**Helpful Scripts:**

Run with `uv` from a git clone (`uv run bin/script.py`) or from the Nextflow assets cache (`uv run ~/.nextflow/assets/Australian-Protein-Design-Initiative/nf-binder-design/bin/script.py`). See `references/setup-and-hpc.md`.

- `bin/get_contigs.py`: Extract contig strings from a hand-cropped PDB structure, for use with `rfd` and `rfd3`.
- `bin/trim_to_contigs.py`: Trim a full structure to specific contig regions — useful to use your `rfd` contigs to prepare the equivalent cropped structure for `bindcraft`, `boltzgen`.
- `bin/renumber_chains.py`: Renumber chain residues sequentially from 1 — useful for `boltzgen` where residues are referred to by 1-based index rather than PDB residue numbers.

## Pre-Flight Checklist

Before running any design method, verify:

1. **Complex size fits VRAM** — target residues + max binder length.
   - 12 GB → ~200–250 residues total
   - 24 GB → ~400 residues total
   - 32 GB → ~550 residues total
  Refer to method-specific reference material for values appropriate for each method.
2. **Hotspot numbering** matches your input PDB (accounting for any cropping and the number scheme [1-based or PDB numbering] used by the method)
3. **No glycans near hotspots** — check PDB LINK records for NAG-ASN or similar bulky post-translational modifications. Check the Uniprot post-translational modification records also.
4. **No exposed hydrophobic core** from unrealistic cropping (or if this is unavoidable, distant from hotspots)
5. **GPU is clear** — check `nvidia-smi` for lingering processes from prior failed runs; kill them before retrying. Don't kill non-binder design processes indiscriminately.
